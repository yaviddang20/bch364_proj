Introduction

Phylogenetic tree representing the supergroups that branch from the last eukaryotic common ancestor (LECA).

Deeply conserved eukaryotic proteins are proteins that diverged early in the eukaryote lineage and remain present in most or all children lineages. These proteins are of high interest due to their ability to maintain a relatively constant function through billions of years of evolution. While most well-conserved proteins in eukaryotes are now known, there remains the question on which protein complexes are deeply conserved in eukaryotes. Knowledge of these complexes can give us a better understanding of how protein interactions have changed or remained the same throughout the evolution of eukaryotes.

KOG groups are orthologous groups that span the entirety of the eukaryotic clade and are specific to eukaryotes. CFMS was performed in previous experiments using 32 different eukaryotic species, producing elution profiles for each KOG group. The elution profiles are used to predict which KOG groups may form complexes, as elution profiles that are very similar or identical indicate that the corresponding KOGs have very similar characteristics and are likely to form complexes. With this knowledge, we can map the KOG groups to a eukaryotic proteome and, using AlphaFold, predict the feasibility of the complex structure in that eukaryote. We then expanded this to include eukaryotic organisms spanning several different clades to determine how well these complexes might be conserved across different eukaryote lineages. For this project, we focused on a subset of eukaryotes, two unicellular eukaryotes (yeast and algae) and a multicellular eukaryote (mouse).
